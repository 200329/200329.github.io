How a Piano Works

Take a peek under the lid of your piano. What you see, is a complicated network of dials, knobs and strings. But really, it isn't that complicated. Basically, it is just a harp, turned on its side, but instead of plucking the strings, there are little hammers that hit the strings instead.

SYour keys are the gateway to the music a piano makes. A key is just a little balance. You put pressure on one side, and it goes down. On the other side, inside the piano, is a hammer, connected to the key. When you press, the hammer strikes the string, making sound. The strings then vibrate, which vibrates the body of the instrument, creating the sound you hear

However, if you press the key really slowly, it doesn't make a sound. This is because there is a small gap between the hammer and the string, called escapement. When you play a key, you are pushing it with enough speed to make up that gap and strike the string. This means, that speed is the main factor in hitting the string, not how deep you push it. If you push a key slowly, when you reach around 2/3 of the way to the bottom, you can feel a bump in the bottom of the key. If you push past the bump, it actually doesn't help you make any additional sound. This means, if you press fast enough, you don't need to press the key all the way down to play the note.

If you keep this in mind, it can make your life a lot easier when playing a fast passage effortlessly, and make refined, pearly tones. It can also help you when playing a glissando, for example, because you don't need to press the key all the way down, and both make it really awkward, and hurt your fingers.
